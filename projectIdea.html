Project Idea

Deep Fake Verification Engine

This project will aim to verify whether or not a video or audio source is computer generated. It will utilise an extension within social media applications which will scan a video or audio source as a user watches or listens. If it detects that the video is a deep fake, it will show a red cross on the bottom of the player, informing the user, or a green tick if the video or audio appears genuine.

With the increasing realism deep fakes are generating, it is now nearly impossible to determine whether or not a video we watch, or audio we listen to, from the internet - or potentially news outlets- is real or fake. In the 2016 US Presidential elections, Trump spent 40% of his advertising budget on online ads, compared to Sanders’ 25% and Clinton’s 6%. In the UK in 2017, of all the political ad spending, 43% was dedicated to online ads. A staggering increase from the 2% spent in 2014 (Kozlowska, 2020). Clearly politicians are using the internet to spread information about their campaign, but it also leaves the door open for others to sow disinformation. This is evidenced by political infographics showing false stats, quotes and everything else, that circulates the social media platforms as truth. Deep fakes have the potential to be used in the same way, however leave out the option of rebuttal, after all if you see a video of someone saying something, it is a lot harder to prove that it, in fact, never happened.

For the remainder of this report, I will refer to video only, however, the same principles can be implemented to audio players. While watching a video, the Deep Fake Verification Engine will scan the data of the video. It will then determine 1 of three possible outcomes. While this is happening, there will be 1 minor graphic that is visible to the user, however it will disappear in the same way the Play/Pause controls disappear from video players. This will include a loading wheel, which would be seen in the top left corner of the player , away from the Play/Pause functions to avoid confusion. It will also be colourised but opaque, as the majority of video players have a grey loading wheel. When the scan is complete it will have 3 outcomes which will all take the place of the loading wheel. The first outcome is a green tick. This will symbolise that the video is genuine and can be trusted and verified. The Green Genuine Tick icon will aim to look like the verification ticks of social media platforms, however will not be blue, as it may mislead people to think that the subject of the video is the one posting, or the one creating the video. The shape of the icon will be familiar to most users and should not confuse users as to the meaning of it. The second outcome will be a Red X icon. The Red X will be the most familiar to users as it is used almost universally to indicate something is not right. This icon will appear if once the video is scanned, and the Deep Fake Verification Engine recognises that the video is not genuine and is computer generated. The engine needs to be certain that the video is computer generated in order for the Red X icon to appear. The third icon that could be generated is that of a yellow question mark. This icon appears when after the Engine runs a scan, it is unknown if the source of the video is genuine or not, and will be called the Second Opinion Question Mark. Along with the icons, there will be a text displayed. For the Green Genuine Tick, the text “Verified Genuine” will appear. For the Red X, “Imitation - not real” and for the Second Opinion Question Mark, the text “Not verified, second opinion needed” will show. The ways that the user will access this technology will eventually be determined by the device which they’re viewing the content on. For smartphones and tablets, it will be accessed through an extension on each of the major social media platforms; Facebook, Twitter, and Youtube. If on a computer, it will be available through a google chrome extension, however, will be redundant if also using the social media apps. After this verification happens, the data will be stored on a server, so the engine can quickly scan to see if the video has already been verified. However, at this stage, a google chrome extension will be the first to be created.

LIRE - an open source image retrieval system running on JAVA would be implemented to determine if the images contained within the program have appeared before on the internet. If a certain number of partial images are contained in the program, then that would suggest that the video would be computer generated. The number would be fine tuned in testing. The Video overlay would be run on JavaScript/HTML. The Deep Fake Verification Engine will be complicated to pull off effectively, so extensive tests, and fine tuning will be required. This may include changing programming languages to find the best combination that will output the results correctly and efficiently.

If this project were to become successful, the result will be the limiting of false information throughout the internet. News outlets and television networks would likely use the program to fact check stories before they are run, while non-partisan commentators and organisations can use the engine to prove credibility and build trust. Finally, the at-home user will be able to determine if something that they are watching is genuine. In the event that deep fakes do not present an authenticity problem, the project can change direction and become an original content identifier. Credit for original work can be given to the proper person/s, and copyright material can be identified and taken down from websites.
